These are different application types with different regulatory frameworks. That is acknowledged. But the underlying question has never changed: can this family build four homes on this lot? The City's own answers — on access, servicing, and site treatment — now contradict each other.
1
Vehicle Access — Reversed

2025 Vehicle access only from Grouse Lane. Front driveways must be removed entirely. The orientation the City previously accepted is now prohibited.
2
The Ditch — Daylight vs. Culvert
2021 Ditch daylighting required. The applicant was told the open watercourse must be maintained and enhanced as a condition of development.
2025 No mention of daylighting. Engineering requirements focus on pipe inspection, CCTV sanitary, and fire flow analysis — implying the ditch can be culverted or is treated as standard drainage infrastructure.
3

2021 Standard rezoning conditions: CAC of $6,000, standard DCCs, straightforward comment letter — 3 pages.
2025 23 planning comments on form and character alone. Full servicing agreement. Water pipe age inspection. Fire flow analysis. The SSMUH pathway — designed by the Province to be simpler — produced a comment letter three times longer and vastly more onerous than the rezoning it replaced.
The constant: one family, one lot, four dwellings, five years. The variable: what the City tells them is required.

SSMUH Was Designed to Reduce Complexity — Not Increase It

The Province introduced SSMUH through Bill 44 specifically to simplify and accelerate small-scale housing delivery. The intent was to make four-unit infill builds accessible to homeowners, not just developers. What is being applied at 1006 Westmount Drive in 2025 is full multifamily-level process: Development Permit, form and character review, facade articulation, bird-friendly design requirements, energy modelling, Step Code compliance. This is the complexity SSMUH was designed to replace.

Pre-Emptive Response

Every Staff Argument — and the Record That Answers It

Staff Position

"The hardship is from the design, not the orientation of the lot." (Michael Olubiyi, Feb 17)

The Record

Architectural drawings A1.2 and A3.11 — on file with the City — show survey elevations of 443.9 ft at Westmount Drive and 423.1 ft at Grouse Lane. The grade is topographic fact. The design responds to it. Reversing the causation does not change the survey data, and it does not change what 34 stairs looks like to a 72-year-old or a parent with a stroller and a bag of groceries.

Staff Position

"Your lot is comparable to other Westmount properties." (Crystal Wickey, Feb 17)

The Record

Crystal Wickey acknowledged on the record that 1006 Westmount has a 6-metre differential and comparable properties have approximately 5 metres. She called this comparable. A 20% difference in grade, on the highest-elevation point on the street, dismissed in one sentence with no site-specific analysis, is not a comparability assessment. It is the absence of one.

Staff Position

"Two driveways erode on-street parking." (Stephen Judd, Feb 17)

The Record

Buildings B1 and B2 are designed with attached front-facing garages. The driveway apron exists in the design regardless of which road it connects to. The on-street parking impact is identical whether the front driveway is permitted or denied. Denying the front connection does not increase available street parking — it only makes the development functionally inaccessible.

Staff Position

"We're being consistent across all SSMUH applications." (Crystal Wickey, Dec 16)

The Record

Consistency with a blanket rule is not compliance with a provision that requires individual assessment. The City's own SSMUH Guide specifies flexibility "on a case-by-case basis" for slope, access, and trees. Bylaw 2937 Section 6.8 contemplates a grade-specific exception applied to individual properties. Uniform application of a rule to all properties, regardless of their topographic characteristics, is not consistent with either document. It is inconsistent with both.

Staff Position

"The variance process would take more time." (framing throughout staff communications)

The Record

This framing was used consistently to discourage the applicant from pursuing relief he is entitled to seek. It is not a response to the merits. It is a deterrent. The applicant has been on this file for four years. Time is not the obstacle. The absence of a written ruling on a straightforward bylaw question is the obstacle — and that can be resolved in this meeting.

The Direct Connection

A staff position that makes intergenerational SSMUH developments physically inaccessible — by requiring elderly residents to climb 34 stairs from a lane to reach their home — is not a neutral enforcement of bylaw language. Bill 44 and Bill 25 — What the Province Requires

Port Moody Cannot Use Its Own Guidelines as a Shield Against Provincial Obligations

The Province did not pass Bill 25 in November 2025 because SSMUH implementation was going well. It passed specifically because municipalities were creating conditions — through parking requirements, access restrictions, and blanket application of site standards — that deterred or made infeasible the housing they were required to permit.

Bill 25 — What the Province Explicitly Targets

Bill 25 amended the Local Government Act to give the Province explicit authority to regulate off-street parking and access requirements that deter SSMUH viability. The UBCM noted in October 2025: "The Province notes that it will monitor site standards and off-street parking requirements for impacts on viability of SSMUH." A policy that makes a four-unit family development with elderly residents practically unlivable is precisely the kind of barrier Bill 25 was designed to address.

At least 3 SSMUH applications stalled on this block alone. One neighbour across the street has already given up entirely. This is how housing doesn't get built — not by denial, but by exhaustion.
The Broader Pattern

When You Make It Impossible, Nobody Builds

This is the mechanism by which the housing does not get built. It does not require a policy that explicitly denies permits. It requires only that the process be difficult enough, expensive enough, and uncertain enough that families who are not developers — who do not have the capital reserves to absorb four years and $250,000 without a permit — give up.

The Compound Burden on This Application — Documented

Lane-only access requirement. Isolated sidewalk slab not required of comparable builds. Parking reduced from 9 to 4 with no transit proximity justification. Utility undergrounding at applicant cost. Collectively, on a file where the City has admittedly applied blanket policy uniformly to a property with site-specific conditions warranting individual assessment, they constitute a pattern.
