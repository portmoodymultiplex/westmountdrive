Two Letters. Same Property. Same Four Dwellings.

In 2021 the City reviewed an RS-1S bare-land strata subdivision for four units. In 2025 they reviewed an SSMUH application — a different regulatory pathway, but the same lot, the same family, and the same goal: four modest homes. The instructions the City gave are fundamentally contradictory.

These are different application types with different regulatory frameworks. That is acknowledged. But the underlying question has never changed: can this family build four homes on this lot? The City's own answers — on access, servicing, and site treatment — now contradict each other.
1
Vehicle Access — Reversed
2021 Driveway access from Westmount Drive was discussed and considered feasible. Front-to-back unit configuration with parking off the front street.
2025 Vehicle access only from Grouse Lane. Front driveways must be removed entirely. The orientation the City previously accepted is now prohibited.
2
The Ditch — Daylight vs. Culvert
2021 Ditch daylighting required. The applicant was told the open watercourse must be maintained and enhanced as a condition of development.
2025 No mention of daylighting. Engineering requirements focus on pipe inspection, CCTV sanitary, and fire flow analysis — implying the ditch can be culverted or is treated as standard drainage infrastructure.
3
Parking — 9 Stalls vs. Maximum 4
2021 Parking per Zoning Bylaw Table 6.3.1 — standard residential calculation. For four units this would yield approximately 9 stalls.
2025 Maximum 4 parking stalls — reduced from the 9 the applicant proposed. The City actively limited parking below what the applicant offered.
4
Process Complexity — Escalation, Not Simplification
2021 Standard rezoning conditions: CAC of $6,000, standard DCCs, straightforward comment letter — 3 pages.
2025 23 planning comments on form and character alone. Full servicing agreement. Water pipe age inspection. Fire flow analysis. The SSMUH pathway — designed by the Province to be simpler — produced a comment letter three times longer and vastly more onerous than the rezoning it replaced.
The constant: one family, one lot, four dwellings, five years. The variable: what the City tells them is required.
