Their Own Document

The City's Staff Report Proves It

On June 18, 2024, the Community Development Department — Policy Planning Division — submitted a report to Council on Bill 44 and SSMUH implementation. The report was authored by people in this building. The person who helped shape it is in this room today. We are not interpreting this document. We are reading it back to you.

"A local government cannot impose regulations in its Zoning Bylaw, or through any other development application approval tools, in such a way that unreasonably prohibits or restricts the use or density."
— Provincial Policy Manual, cited verbatim in Port Moody's own June 18, 2024 staff report to Council
The Core Problem
Port Moody has technically complied with Bill 44. But operationally, it is undermining it.
1
Process Is Too Slow
18–24 month timelines kill small-scale housing. Families without developer capital cannot absorb this. This application is now in year five.
2
Costs Are Too High
The 2025 staff comments letter demands: full servicing agreement, 63-year-old water pipe inspection, 64-year-old sewer CCTV, fire flow analysis, geotechnical report, construction management plan — requirements exceeding those of full subdivisions.
3
Interpretation Is Too Rigid
Blanket parking maximum of 4 — even when the applicant proposed 9. Blanket single-access from lane — even with a 20.8-foot grade drop. No case-by-case assessment despite the City's own SSMUH Guide promising one.
"You may be technically permitting it on paper. In practice, you are restricting it."

The Proof Is in Your Own Letters

In 2021, staff required 3 items before Council — updated site plan, arborist report, servicing concept. Andrei Pop wrote: "We look forward to your revised submission." The tone was collaborative. The path was clear.

In 2025, for the same lot and the same four dwellings, staff produced 23 planning comments, required 22 engineering drawings, and demanded a Fire Department Access Plan, Construction Management Plan, Preconstruction Infrastructure Condition assessment, knotweed management plan, and bird-window collision mitigation strategy. The SSMUH pathway — designed by the Province to be simpler — produced a process orders of magnitude harder than the rezoning it replaced.

One Barrier Removed. Six Created.

The Province removed public hearings for SSMUH to eliminate friction. Port Moody quietly replaced that one barrier with six new ones:

1
Development Permit Area 6 (DPA 6)
2
SSMUH Development Guidelines Manual
3
OCP updates to embed those guidelines
4
Updated ACC, DCC & CAC programs
5
Updated Development Approval Procedures Bylaw
6
Updated Subdivision, Servicing & Building Bylaws

Every one of those is a gate an applicant must pass through. Staff essentially rebuilt the old approval structure under a new name. The staff report itself acknowledged this architecture — and the people in this room built it.

The DP Requirement Was a Choice — Not an Obligation

From the staff report: "SSMUH is a form of multi-family housing and, like all other multi-family housing in Port Moody, staff believe that SSMUH should be subject to a form and character development permit."

The word "believe" is the opening. This was a discretionary staff decision, not a legislative requirement. The Province explicitly discourages form-and-character development permits for SSMUH. Yet staff built an entire DPA around it anyway — and the October 8, 2025 comments letter for 1006 Westmount now uses that DPA to impose 23 separate planning requirements on a 4-unit family project.

Meanwhile, the September 18, 2025 letter for 348 Valour Drive — processed under the exact same DPA framework, 20 days earlier — took a collaborative approach. The Fire Department had "no issues." Building had 2 comments. Staff complimented the applicant's design choices. Same DPA. Same City. Different treatment.

The question for this room: on what legal basis did staff impose a DP requirement the Province actively discouraged, and who authorized the inconsistent application of that requirement across files?

"SSMUH may reduce incentives for land assembly and larger multi-family development."
If SSMUH is already weakening larger development, the last thing the City should be doing is making small-scale projects harder too. This applicant is the exact developer the City should be supporting — the middle ground between no development and large rezonings. And the 1022 Westmount vacant lot — a decade of nothing, one owner financially ruined — is what happens when the City makes it impossible for everyone.
