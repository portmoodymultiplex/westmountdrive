Why This Matters

A Community That Needs This Housing

“We'll accept applications, but it's not something we'd be advertising. It's not something we wanted to see.” — City planning staff to Dawar Zada, regarding SSMUH

The Glenayre Pre-School

When Dawar's four-year-old started pre-K at the Glenayre Centre, he noticed something: out of 13 children in the class, only 3 were from the neighbourhood. The other 10 families were commuting in from outside the area. When he asked why, the answer was the same: lack of suitable housing they could afford. They didn't want aging ranchers. They wanted homes built for modern families.

The Seniors Who Won't Leave

Meanwhile, the seniors who do live in the neighbourhood told Dawar the opposite problem: they don't want condos. They'd rather die in their homes. They're going to extreme lengths to modify aging ranchers just to avoid having to move.

Homes in the Glenayre neighbourhood modified with accessibility ramps — seniors choosing to retrofit rather than leave a community with no suitable alternatives.

Taking It to the People

When the City chose not to inform residents about their new housing options, Dawar built the infrastructure himself. He launched PortMoodyMultiplex.ca — a full-service resource for homeowners exploring what's possible on their lot — and door-knocked nearly 1,000 homes across Port Moody to share what staff wouldn't.

Community Resource
PortMoodyMultiplex.ca
Free feasibility studies, interactive maps, consulting — built so homeowners don't have to navigate the process alone.

Hosting Public Education Events

Beyond the website, Dawar organized free community information sessions — bringing in Lightwell Homes, a Vancouver developer specializing in missing middle housing, to walk residents through the policy, the process, and what's actually possible on their land. He personally invited City staff to attend, so residents could hear directly from both sides.

Community Event
“Your Home, Your Future” — a free info session at the Glenayre Centre, open to all residents.
City Invitation
Direct email to City staff inviting them to attend and engage with residents in person.
None of this outreach was required. None of it was prompted by the City. It was done because residents deserved to know what options existed for their homes — and no one else was telling them.
