The February 2026 Incident

1
The Meeting

Dawar calls a meeting to discuss dual access. Presents documented arguments: the 20.8-foot grade differential, the 932 Westmount discrepancy, the bylaw exception in Section 6.8. Engineering Manager Stephen Judd could not immediately cite the specific bylaw provision — admits on record: "I have to look at the wording again." Staff say they need to "go back and double-check." Meeting ends with no resolution.

2
One Month of Silence

No response. No email. No phone call. An applicant who has been waiting five years is made to wait again — because staff needed to verify their own bylaw.

3
The Follow-Up Email

Dawar emails staff requesting an update. Days pass. Still nothing.

4
The Office Visit

Dawar goes to City Hall in person. Crystal Wickey, the assigned planner, is dismissive — asks why he couldn't just wait for an email. Dawar responds that he has been waiting for a month.

Crystal had the answer ready. It was already prepared. They had been sitting on the response the entire time, requiring the applicant to physically retrieve information that should have been proactively communicated.

5
The Answer

Despite strong arguments about dual access, the 932 discrepancy, and the bylaw interpretation staff had to "double-check" — the answer was no. No written rationale. No legal citation. No explanation of why the grade-based exception did not apply.

6
The Variance Misdirection

Crystal tells Dawar the only other option is to "seek a variance" — without specifying which process. There are two paths:

Board of Variance
Costly, adversarial, no staff support, long timeline
This is what Dawar was steered toward.
Council Variance
Faster, with staff recommendation possible
This option was never disclosed.

By saying only "variance," staff steered the applicant toward the most expensive, most time-consuming, and least likely-to-succeed path — while concealing an alternative that could have resolved the issue months ago.
