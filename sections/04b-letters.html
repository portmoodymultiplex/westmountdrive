Two Letters. Same Property. Same Four Dwellings.

In 2021 the City reviewed an RS-1S bare-land strata subdivision for four units. In 2025 they reviewed an SSMUH application — a different regulatory pathway, but the same lot, the same family, and the same goal: four modest homes. The instructions the City gave are fundamentally contradictory.

These are different application types with different regulatory frameworks. That is acknowledged. But the underlying question has never changed: can this family build four homes on this lot? The City's own answers — on access, servicing, and site treatment — now contradict each other.
1
Vehicle Access — Reversed
2021 Driveway from Westmount was discussed and permitted. A maximum 6m wide driveway on Westmount Drive was allowed. Staff noted the driveway for Lot 1 should be separate from the common pedestrian access — not eliminated entirely.
2025 Vehicle access from Westmount is now prohibited. Both proposed front driveways must be removed. All access must come from Grouse Lane via a single driveway of up to 15m — on a steep, narrow lane with a 20-foot grade differential.
2
The Ditch — Supported Variance vs. Ignored
2021 Staff noted the QEP's recommended alternative top-of-bank and wrote: "Staff are supportive of the variance given the rationale provided." The ditch was recognized as a Class C watercourse. Staff actively assisted the applicant toward a workable solution.
2025 Ditch to be reinstated and culvert installed at the driveway crossing from Grouse Lane — at developer's expense. CCTV inspection of 64-year-old sewer pipe required. The collaborative posture on the ditch disappeared entirely.
3
Parking — 9 Stalls vs. Maximum 4
2021 Parking per Zoning Bylaw Table 6.3.1: two spaces per principal dwelling, one per secondary suite. For four units the standard calculation yielded approximately 9 stalls.
2025 Maximum 4 parking stalls — reduced from the 9 currently proposed. The City actively limited parking below what the applicant offered and below what their own previous calculation required.
4
Process Complexity — 3 Items vs. 23+
2021 Three items required before Council consideration: (1) updated site plan with setbacks and parking, (2) updated arborist report, (3) updated servicing concept plan. CAC of $6,000 net. Standard DCCs. That was it.
2025 23 planning comments. 22 engineering drawings required. Full servicing agreement. 63-year-old water pipe inspection. 64-year-old sewer CCTV inspection. Fire Department Access Plan. Construction Management Plan. Preconstruction infrastructure assessment. Geotechnical report. Knotweed management plan. Bird-window collision mitigation. The pathway designed by the Province to be simpler produced a letter orders of magnitude more onerous than the rezoning it replaced.
5
Tone — Collaborative vs. Adversarial
2021 Andrei Pop signed off with: "We look forward to your revised submission." Staff were "supportive" of the ditch variance. The letter was structured to move the file forward. The relationship was professional and constructive.
2025 No such warmth. Requirements piled with no path to resolution offered. Design aesthetic critiqued ("unsightly walls," massing critique). Entire orientation reversed with no written explanation or transition support. The applicant went from a partner to an obstacle.
The constant: one family, one lot, four dwellings, four years. The variable: what the City tells them is required — and whether they want to help.
