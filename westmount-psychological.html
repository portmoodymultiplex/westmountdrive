Background

The Applicant

Dawar Zada is a long-time Port Moody resident, realtor, and father of three young children. He purchased 1006 Westmount Drive in 2021 with the intention of building a home for his family while also creating additional housing that would allow others to remain in the community.

The original plan was to complete a subdivision and begin construction within approximately one year. Since then, the project has evolved in response to regulatory changes, including the introduction of Small-Scale Multi-Unit Housing (SSMUH) under provincial legislation.

Over the past four years, the project has progressed through multiple applications, design revisions, and technical requirements. Throughout this process, the goal has remained consistent: to deliver housing that supports families, accommodates aging in place, and fits within the character of the neighbourhood.

What These Requirements Actually Mean

45 requirements did not just slow this project down.
They made it effectively impossible for the people who would actually want to live here.

Glenayre is not a neighbourhood of developers. It is a neighbourhood of young families who grew up here and are trying to stay, and seniors who have lived here for decades and are trying to age in place. These are not people with development teams, investor capital, or the capacity to absorb years of iterative revisions, geotechnical reports, knotweed management plans, and bird collision strategies on top of an already complex application.

The 45 requirements in the October 8 letter — submitted in response to what the Province explicitly designed as a simplified housing pathway — represent a cost and timeline burden that would deter most private homeowners from proceeding. A family with three young children does not have the resources to fund another round of redesigns, another set of consultant reports, another year of carrying costs on a $2.125M property with no income. That is not a hypothetical. It is this family's reality right now.

When SSMUH applications become indistinguishable from full subdivision applications in terms of complexity and cost — when the "easy" route requires more than the hard one — the policy has failed in its implementation. The Province created SSMUH because municipalities were doing exactly this: making development so burdensome that real residents could not access it. Port Moody is doing it again, in real time, on this file.

Our Neighbours Got Dual Access.
The Bylaw Entitles Us to the Same.

932 & 1006 Westmount — same block, 80 metres apart

The owners at 932 Westmount are not strangers. They are neighbours. They are friends. They went through their own difficult, frustrating process dealing with the City — and they got through it. When Dawar asked them directly about how dual access was approved on their property, they told him straight up: they persuaded their way into it.

That is not how a bylaw is supposed to work. Bylaw 2937 Section 6.8 provides a grade exception for exactly this situation. It is not a discretionary favour to be negotiated. It is a written provision that exists precisely because elevation differentials like the ones on this street make lane-only access unreasonable. If one neighbour had to persuade their way into something the bylaw already entitled them to — and another neighbour is being denied that same entitlement without a written reason — then the problem is not the bylaw. The problem is how the bylaw is being applied, and to whom.

The City cannot simultaneously claim that 932 was a forced exception while refusing the same outcome at a property with greater elevation, greater constraints, and four years more file history. A precedent has been set. The only question is whether the City will apply it consistently — or selectively.

Dual Access and Bylaw Application

The Remaining Issue on This File Is Limited to Access.

The Port Moody Zoning Bylaw (2937, Section 6.8) provides flexibility where site conditions, including elevation, make standard access impractical. This provision exists to allow for case-by-case evaluation based on real site constraints.

At 1006 Westmount, there is a 20.8 ft (6m) elevation difference between the lane and the street. Lane-only access results in significant vertical travel and distance to entry. The surrounding street pattern includes front access on all homes, with several properties incorporating dual access — including 932 Westmount, approved under similar conditions. Given these factors, the request for dual access is based on site-specific conditions and alignment with both bylaw intent and existing precedent.

Process Clarity

Communication of Available Pathways

Throughout the application, certain options and pathways were not clearly outlined in early stages of the process. For applicants navigating SSMUH — many of whom are homeowners rather than developers — clear communication of available options, including variance pathways, is important to ensure informed decision-making.

Dawar had been waiting for more than a month for a written response to the dual access question — the single outstanding issue on an application that had already been active for four years. No response had come. He went to City Hall in person to get one.

When he arrived, Crystal Wickey was at her desk. She had her legs up. She looked up and asked him — abruptly — why he couldn't just wait for an email.

He explained that he had been waiting for over a month. She already had the response prepared. The answer was no. The same no that had never been committed to writing during the meeting, never been explained with reference to a specific bylaw provision, and never been accompanied by any indication of what options he had if he disagreed.

After Dawar walked her through the evidence — the bylaw, the precedent at 932, the elevation survey, the Valour comparison — the answer remained no. When he asked directly: "What are my next steps if I don't agree with this decision?"

Her response: "Seek a variance."

That was it. No explanation of the variance process. No reference to the Board of Variance. No mention that this option existed in any prior correspondence, any letter, or any meeting. In four years of file history, the option of seeking a variance through Council had never been communicated to the applicant in writing. It was disclosed in passing, in a one-word answer, after being directly asked for next steps.

This is not a minor procedural detail. Homeowners navigating SSMUH applications are not development lawyers. They rely on City staff to explain the process and the options available to them. Withholding pathways — even passively, through omission — creates an uneven playing field that disadvantages the applicants who can least afford to find out what they didn't know.

Dawar recorded the encounter. Given the pattern of undocumented reversals, shifting requirements, and verbal commitments that later disappeared from the file, he had no other choice. The recording is part of the evidentiary record.

What Was Withheld

In four years of active file correspondence — letters, emails, and recorded meetings — the applicant was never informed in writing that a variance through Council was an available pathway. It was disclosed verbally, in a single word, only when he asked directly what to do if he disagreed. Staff are expected to explain all available options. That did not happen here.

The Provincial Mandate

The Province created a pathway.
Port Moody was required to open it.

Bill 44 passed in 2023. Small-Scale Multi-Unit Housing. The Province removed public hearings. Removed the need for Council votes. Created a staff-led, streamlined process specifically designed to make four-unit housing accessible to homeowners — not just developers — in single-family neighbourhoods across BC.

Dawar had been fighting a subdivision application for years. When SSMUH passed, he saw what it was designed to be: a shorter path. He submitted his application with optimism. He thought: this is finally it.

Provincial Policy Manual — What the Law Requires

"A local government cannot impose regulations in its Zoning Bylaw, or through any other development application approval tools, in such a way that unreasonably prohibits or restricts the use or density."

Provincial Framework

Aligning Local Process
with Provincial Intent

SSMUH was introduced through Bill 44 to create a more accessible and streamlined pathway for small-scale housing. Bill 25 further clarified that local governments should not impose requirements that make these developments financially or practically unworkable. The intent of this framework is to support the delivery of housing that is achievable for homeowners, not just large-scale developers. Aligning local processes with this intent is key to ensuring successful implementation. The compliance deadline is June 30, 2026.

Provision What It Requires Status at 1006 Westmount
Bill 44 (2023) Municipalities must permit 3-4 unit SSMUH on eligible lots Technically complied — application is permissible
Bill 25 (2025) Prohibits requirements that make SSMUH financially or practically infeasible Lane-only access requiring 34 stairs is a practical barrier. Compliance deadline: June 30, 2026
Bylaw 2937 s.6.8 Alternative street access permitted where grade makes lane access infeasible Exception cited but never applied. No written rationale provided. Applied at 932 Westmount — not at 1006

Provincial Policy Manual Cannot impose regulations that unreasonably restrict density Cited in Port Moody's own June 2024 staff report. Applied to the letter at 348 Valour. Not applied here
Port Moody has technically complied with Bill 44. But operationally, it is undermining it. The Province did not pass Bill 25 because SSMUH implementation was going well. It passed specifically to address what is being done at 1006 Westmount Drive.
