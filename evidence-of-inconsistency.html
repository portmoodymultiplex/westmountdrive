I
The Evidence
What follows is not opinion. It is a documented record of inconsistency, contradiction, and selective enforcement — drawn from the City's own letters, the City's own staff, and the City's own bylaws.
Chapter One

A Family That Did Everything Right

In 2021, Dawar Zada purchased 1006 Westmount Drive for $2,125,000 and moved his young family in — his wife, three children under six, and an aging father — expecting to live there for one year while permits were processed. That was nearly four years ago.

He started with a subdivision application. It was complex, expensive, and slow — but staff were professional. In March 2021, the City sent a comments letter with 3 items required before Council. A driveway from Westmount Drive was discussed and permitted. Staff wrote: "We look forward to your revised submission." The tone was collaborative. The path was clear.

Then Bill 44 passed. The Province created SSMUH — a streamlined pathway designed to cut through exactly the kind of friction Dawar had been fighting. No more public hearings. No more Council votes. A staff-led process to make four-unit housing accessible to families, not just developers. Dawar submitted his SSMUH application with real optimism. He thought: this is finally it.

What He Got Back

23 planning comments. 22 engineering drawings. Full servicing agreement. Inspection of a 63-year-old water pipe. CCTV of a 64-year-old sewer. Fire Department Access Plan. Geotechnical report. Knotweed plan. Bird-window collision mitigation. And a complete reversal: both front driveways eliminated, all access forced through a rear lane 20.8 feet below street grade.

The pathway the Province created to make housing easier had produced a process orders of magnitude harder than the subdivision it replaced.

Same property. Same four homes. Same family.

The Question This Raises

These are not different bylaws. These are not different cities. They are the same application type, the same regulatory framework, the same month — and the outcome was determined entirely by which staff member processed the file. Port Moody's own SSMUH Guide promises case-by-case assessment. 348 Valour received it. 1006 Westmount received a blanket policy — confirmed on the record by the assigned planner herself.

Why the "Different Application Type" Argument Fails

The physical facts are identical. Bylaw 2937 Section 6.8 provides a grade exception for alternative street access — regardless of application type. The grade at 1006 exceeds that at 932. The exception applies more strongly here, not less.

The access standard on this street is established. Every home on Westmount Drive accesses from Westmount. Six have dual access. Zero have lane-only. Requiring 1006 to be the first — at the steepest point — without written justification is not the application of a bylaw. It is the arbitrary exercise of discretion inconsistent with the established pattern of the street.

Bill 25 explicitly prohibits this. The Province's November 2025 amendments removed municipal authority to impose access conditions that make SSMUH practically infeasible. A requirement that forces elderly residents and young children to climb 34 stairs from their garage to their front door is precisely the barrier Bill 25 was enacted to address. Compliance deadline: June 30, 2026.

"932 Westmount — it's kind of straddling when the provincial mandates came out and we were kind of handcuffed as a city in terms of that particular application."
Patrick Byrne · Engineering · December 16, 2025
932 received dual access. We want the same. The bylaw hasn't changed. The street hasn't changed. The grade hasn't changed. What changed is the staff member processing the file.
Chapter Five

We Already Know What Lane-Only Access Does.

A multiplex project off Mount Royal Drive was directed to lane-only access — the same restriction being pushed at 1006 Westmount. We know the builders. We know what they went through. And we know what happened after.

The result: 11 vehicles between two homes. Most parked on the street and in the neighbouring lane. A blind corner has been created. Two accidents have already occurred. It is the single biggest complaint in the surrounding neighbourhood.

Mount Royal Drive — what lane-only access looks like once families move in
Staff's Response When Shown This

Dawar raised the Mount Royal situation in a recorded meeting. Staff's response was to call it a "terrible example" — dismissing the real-world consequences of their own policy rather than reconsidering it. They then insisted the same restriction apply at 1006 Westmount and demanded parking be limited further still — engineering the exact same problem on a lot with an even more extreme grade drop.

Both Streets Oppose This

Residents from both Westmount Drive and Grouse Lane understand what lane-only access means — they've watched it play out on Mount Royal. They support this project. They want this housing built. What they don't support is a planning decision that creates the exact problem they've already seen unfold one street over. The petition was unambiguous. Several residents are prepared to file formal complaints.
